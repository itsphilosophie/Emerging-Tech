The Company

Ford Motor Company is a renowned American automaker based in Dearborn, Michigan in the suburbs of Detroit. It is a world’s leading multinational company founded in 1903 by Henry Ford. The history of Ford is marked by extraordinary innovations such as the Quadricycle, followed by the famous Model A as well as the revolutionary implementation of the assembly line in Ford’s factories on a wide scale, which contributed enormously to improving the efficiency of production. Nevertheless, in the last two decades Ford shows little progress on its digitization. With the enormous growth of the electric transportation industry in recent years, the company plans to capitalize on the industry’s transformation. While Ford already attempted to digitize its business, the earlier efforts turned out to be unsuccessful. Therefore, in order to analyze Ford’s current situation and outline the vision and strategy roadmap, firstly we shall look at the current state of play and Ford’s business environment.  

Ford is one of the world’s leading automotive brands. Even though Ford operates in 62 countries globally, the United States is by far Ford’s largest market, accounting for more than 60% of the Company’s total revenue in 2015. The Company’s other large national markets are the UK, Canada and Germany (Ford Motor Company, 2016). As of January 2022, Ford has a market cap of $82.52 Billion (Ford, 2022). The company’s current assets include 90 factories, two automotive brands—Ford and Lincoln—and the Ford Motor Credit Company (PESTLE analysis Contribution, 2015). Ford serves a range of customers within the automotive sector, with its end-users largely comprising general consumers and commercial businesses. The majority of Ford’s sales of vehicles, parts and accessories are made to its worldwide network of Ford and Lincoln dealerships. The company also sells vehicles to automotive and parts distributors and commercial fleet companies, such as car rental providers, government bodies, and industrial and contracting companies (Ford Motor Company, 2016). 

When it comes to market segmentation, Ford’s demographics show that the company focuses on women, the younger generation and on people with average income. Also, it is beneficial for Ford to make the young people aware about their eco-friendly automobiles, because young people are one of Ford’s target markets (Balawardhana, 2020). Ford’s product line-up includes various sedans, SUVs (sport utility vehicles) such as Explorer and legendary Bronco series, pick-up trucks (e.g. F-150) and performance-oriented Mustang. What is more, Ford’s 2021 product line-up includes various hybrid and electric vehicles including: Mustang Mach-E, Escape Hybrid, F-150 Hybrid and Explorer Limited Hybrid. In 2018 Ford announced an investment plan that aims at digital transformation of Ford, which included $4 billion investment in a new self-driving business unit over the following 5 years. Dubbed Ford Autonomous Vehicles LLC, the segment will house all of Ford's autonomous driving efforts moving forward, including engineering and systems integration, business strategy, and fleet management (Shields, 2018). 

When it comes to availability, Ford allows customers to view its automotive products online, as well as build personalized models of its cars on the company’s website, however customers are not able to purchase these products on a self-service basis.

They can, nevertheless, apply for credit and financing on a self-service basis, sales though must be completed through Ford Credit representatives. Ford sells its products primarily through independent dealerships, thus maintains close relationships, consulting with these customers and providing various support services. Ford also provides customisable lease plans that can be tailored to meet the individual needs of its commercial clients.

It offers support and maintenance services, including extended service contracts, and provides an online chat support service. It also provides support to its financing clients over the phone, online and by mail (Ford Motor Company, 2016). 

Blue Tag

The new pricing strategy of Ford Motor Company is called “Blue Tag”. For instance, the pricing of Ford Fiesta differs between 6% - 15%. Penetration pricing and price skimming are the two known pricing strategies that are being followed by Ford. The Competitors

Main competitors

Ford’s main competitors include: Fiat Chrysler Automobiles, General Motors Company, Honda Motor Company, Hyundai-Kia Automotive Group, PSA Peugeot Citroën, Renault-Nissan B.V., Suzuki Motor Corporation, Toyota Motor Corporation, Volkswagen AG Group and many other automotive companies (Jurevicius, 2018).

Electric market

The fast-growing segment of the electric auto business dominated by Tesla made well-established Ford and VW very much electrically oriented when it comes to the future development of the companies.

Ford's opportunity

While clearly, Ford’s market cap is far cry from Tesla’s $933.96 billion capitalisation, the Mach-E model series will be Ford’s first big test on whether the company can compete with the likes of Tesla for an E.V. customer. Especially, the lower price of Ford’s cars might attract customers who were previously discouraged from buying into the electric car segment (Boudette, 2021). Mustang Mach-E retail sales jumped 76.9 percent in October of 2021, with 21,703 vehicles sold year to date (Kane, 2021).

Nevertheless, those numbers are still far away from Tesla’s 2021 sales, which accounted for 936 000 vehicles last year, up 87% from its 2020 sales (Deutsche Welle, 2022). 

The Opportunities

When it comes to digital technology, Ford announced an $11 billion investment in a corporate restructuring to digitally transform itself over the coming decade. Among its ambitions, Ford plans to invest in electric car tech, which should ease the transition to AVs (autonomous vehicles) since self-driving systems work better with electric drivetrains than internal combustion engines (Shields, 2018).

Nevertheless, a year after the announcement of the revolutionizing investment was made, it turned out the new segment called Ford Smart Mobility, which was developed to build digitally enabled cars with enhanced mobility, is a failure (Morgan, 2019). The service was separate from the rest of the company instead of integrating digital solutions. At the same time, while investing millions of dollars into the digital technology programmes, Ford failed to effectively communicate their goals, strategy, purpose and outlook with their employees (Kitani, 2019). As of 2021, Ford unveiled an alliance with Google to employ its cloud computing platform.

The Google venture is not "exclusive" and Ford's vehicles would also employ Amazon's voice program and other programs by Apple and Microsoft, which shows how important digital technology solutions are for the company in terms of Ford’s development (Ford Media Center, 2021). On the other hand, 2021 and 2022 are years marked by the supply chain issues for the automotive industry, which relies heavily on semiconductors. Nearly 10 million fewer cars were built in 2021 due to supply chain issues and a semiconductor shortage (Ashworth, 2021). In September 2021, Ford’s revenue fell about 5 percent, to $35.7 billion. In the same period last year, when auto sales were rebounding after a slump caused by the coronavirus pandemic, Ford earned $2.4 billion on $37.5 billion in revenue. Ford’s CFO shared that he expects the constraints on chips to remain fluid through 2022 and could extend into 2023, but the expectation is that the severity will reduce (Boudette, 2021).
